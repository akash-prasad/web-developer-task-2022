Queering identity

Queer theory and activism have responded to discrimination and hatred by taking a word that had historically been used to insult gay people - 'queer' - and reclaiming it as a radical tool. But queer isn't just about claiming justice or freedom or tolerance or acceptance for particular identity categories, but about resisting dominant forces, undermining majority positions.

In 1992, film critic B. Ruby Rich coined the term 'new queer cinema' to describe a generation of queer filmmakers that 'poured its heart and soul, shock and anger into modes of representation capable of spinning new styles out of old and new genres out of those that had worn out'. Rich, B. Ruby, New Queer Cinema: The Director's Cut (Durham, NC: Duke University Press, 2013), p. 13.The 'queer' films released in this period - such as Todd Haynes's Poison and Cheryl Dunye's Watermelon Woman are 'irreverent, energetic [...] and excessive. And above all, they're full of pleasure'.Rich, B. Ruby, New Queer Cinema: The Director's Cut (Durham, NC: Duke University Press, 2013), p. 18.

Queer isn't just about trying to advocate for gay rights within the existing political system but about creating oppositional positions.
